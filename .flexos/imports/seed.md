## 1. Vision

You’ve been studying Spanish for two years. You have a 400-day streak on a popular green owl app. You know how to say "The apple is red" and "I would like to purchase a train ticket." 

Then, your friend from Mexico City sends you a meme on Instagram. You recognize the verbs. You know the nouns. But the sentence makes absolutely zero sense. It’s a pun, wrapped in local slang, referencing a cultural moment you missed. You paste it into Google Translate. Google spits out a literal translation that is even more confusing. You are, for all intents and purposes, functionally illiterate. 

This is the gap in language learning. Textbooks teach you how to survive as a tourist. The internet teaches you how to connect as a human. But until now, there hasn't been a bridge between the two.

Lingo.jpg is that bridge. It’s a gamified language learning platform where the curriculum is curated entirely from native internet culture. Instead of matching "gato" to a picture of a cat, you are deciphering viral tweets, TikTok comment sections, and localized meme formats. 

When you successfully translate a meme—and understand *why* it's funny—you earn points. You spend those points in The Vault to unlock custom, culturally specific emojis and stickers that you can export to your phone's keyboard. You aren't just learning a language; you are earning the right to participate in its culture.

This is not for passing the DELE or the JLPT. This is for the 11pm scroller who wants to laugh at foreign TikToks without checking the comments for an explanation. It is built on the premise that humor is the ultimate test of fluency. If you can understand a culture's memes, you understand their language.

## 2. Features

### The Feed (Your Curriculum)
There are no "Lessons" or "Units." There is only The Feed. It looks like Instagram or TikTok, but every post is a meme in your target language. The text is partially obscured or interactive. Your job is to translate the punchline, fill in the missing slang, or match the idiom to its English equivalent. It’s the addictive scroll of social media, weaponized for education.

### The Culture Breakdown
This is the magic trick. When you solve a meme (or give up), the AI steps in. It doesn't just give you the literal translation. It gives you the "Culture Breakdown." It explains that *“Wey, no mames”* literally translates to “Dude, don’t suck,” but practically means “Bro, no way” or “You’ve got to be kidding me.” It explains the origin of the joke, the tone (is it aggressive? sarcastic? affectionate?), and how to use it in a sentence without sounding like a boomer.

### The Vault
Gamification works when the rewards actually matter. Nobody cares about a digital gold star. They care about flexing. As you translate memes, you earn Glot Points. You spend these points in The Vault to unlock custom, localized emojis and sticker packs (e.g., the "Cheems" dog with Spanish text, or Japanese reaction faces). Once unlocked, you can use them in the app's community sections or export them to WhatsApp/iMessage. 

### The Deck
Every meme you translate successfully gets saved to your Deck. This is your personalized, contextual flashcard library. Instead of reviewing abstract words, you are reviewing the exact meme where you learned the word. The image triggers the memory of the joke, which triggers the vocabulary. It’s spaced repetition disguised as a meme gallery.

### Field Notes (UGC)
Found a meme in the wild that you don't understand? Upload it to Field Notes. The Lingo.jpg AI will run OCR to extract the text, break down the slang, explain the joke to you, and then format it into a playable challenge for the rest of the community. The curriculum builds itself from the collective scrolling habits of the users.

## 3. Pages

You open the app and bypass the boring stuff. The landing page is a live, interactive meme challenge. "Translate this to enter." It proves the value immediately. Once you create an account, you land directly in **The Feed**. It’s a dark, immersive interface. A meme takes up the center of the screen. Below it, a scrambled sentence or a fill-in-the-blank input. You make your guess, the screen flashes neon green for correct, and the **Culture Breakdown** drawer slides up to explain the joke. You swipe up to go to the next one. 

In the bottom navigation, the center tab is a glowing padlock icon: **The Vault**. Tapping it takes you to a neon-lit storefront. Here, you see silhouettes of locked emojis. A counter at the top shows your Glot Points. You tap a locked "Crying Cat (Spanish Edition)" sticker, spend 500 points, and the unlock animation plays. 

The **Deck** page is your library. It looks like a photo gallery, but tapping any meme flips it over to reveal the vocabulary, the slang definitions, and your mastery level of those specific words. 

The **Profile** page is your gamer tag. It shows your current streak, your total points, your top three unlocked emojis displayed proudly next to your avatar, and a heat map of your learning activity over the last year.

## 4. Data

The universe of Lingo.jpg revolves around the `Meme`. A Meme isn't just an image; it's a puzzle. It contains the raw image, the original text, the obscured text (the part the user has to guess), and the literal translation. 

Attached to every Meme is a `Breakdown`. This is the AI-generated or human-curated cultural context. It contains the explanation of the joke, definitions of specific slang terms used, and a tone indicator.

Users interact with Memes by creating `Attempts`. An Attempt records whether they guessed the translation correctly, how long it took, and how many hints they used. This data feeds into the spaced repetition algorithm for their `Deck`.

The economy runs on `Points` and `Unlocks`. Users earn points through Attempts. They spend points on `Emojis`. An Emoji is a digital asset (a GIF or PNG) with a localized meaning. When a user buys one, an `Unlock` record is created, linking the User to the Emoji, allowing them to use it in the `Comments` section of any Meme.

## 5. Flows

### The "Aha" Moment
You're scrolling The Feed in bed. A Spanish meme appears. It’s a picture of a dog looking exhausted. The text says: *"Yo después de decir 'no mames' 50 veces en una hora."* The phrase 'no mames' is blanked out. You have to translate "You've got to be kidding me" from English into the blank. 

You type it in. The screen shakes slightly, a satisfying *ding* plays, and the text turns neon green. +50 Points. The Culture Breakdown slides up. It explains that while it literally means something vulgar, in Mexico it's the standard punctuation mark for disbelief. You laugh because you finally *get* it. You tap the bookmark icon to save it to your Deck, and swipe up to the next meme.

### The Vault Flex
You’ve hit a 7-day streak. The streak multiplier just deposited 1,000 Glot Points into your account. You navigate to The Vault. You've been eyeing the "Chilaquiles Reaction" sticker for days. It costs 800 points. You tap it. A loud, arcade-style unlock animation plays. The sticker is yours. 

You immediately go back to The Feed, find a meme about breakfast, open the comments, and drop your newly unlocked Chilaquiles sticker. Two other users reply to your comment with fire emojis. You didn't just learn a word; you bought status in the community.

### The Field Note Upload
You're on Twitter. You see a viral tweet in Japanese that has 100k likes, but you have no idea why it's funny. You screenshot it. You open Lingo.jpg, go to Field Notes, and upload the image. 

A loading state says "Decoding the culture..." After 5 seconds, the AI breaks it down: it identifies the slang, explains that it's a reference to a recent political gaffe, and provides the English equivalent. The app asks: "Turn this into a challenge?" You hit yes. The meme enters the global feed. Whenever someone else solves it, you get a 10% cut of the points they earn. You are now a curriculum contributor.

## 6. Design

Lingo.jpg does not look like a classroom. It looks like the internet. 

The aesthetic is heavily inspired by late-night scrolling. Dark mode is not an option; it is the default and only mode. The background is a deep, bruised purple-black, allowing the memes (which are often bright, low-res, or colorful) to pop off the screen. 

The accents are pure cyber-neon. A toxic, radioactive green serves as the primary action color—it's the color of correct answers, high scores, and active streaks. A vibrant electric purple serves as the secondary accent for The Vault and premium interactions. 

Typography is split between a bold, hyper-legible sans-serif for UI elements, and a slightly chaotic, marker-style display font for streak counters and point callouts. The text needs to feel digital but slightly raw, like a Discord server run by hackers who love linguistics.

Components are designed to get out of the way of the content. Cards have no borders, only subtle ambient shadows that glow in the accent colors. Buttons are chunky, pill-shaped, and feature aggressive haptic feedback. When you get a translation right, the button doesn't just change color; the phone buzzes, the points physically fly up the screen, and the UI reacts. 

The Empty States are snarky. If you have no saved memes, the app tells you: "Your deck is as empty as a textbook. Go scroll."

## 7. Technical

The app needs to feel snappy, native, and highly visual. Because it relies heavily on images and real-time interactions, performance is paramount. It doesn't need to be a native app immediately; a highly optimized PWA (Progressive Web App) solves the distribution problem and allows for rapid iteration, which is crucial when your content is based on fast-moving internet trends.

The AI layer is the secret weapon. Standard translation APIs (like Google Translate) fail spectacularly at slang and memes. The app requires an LLM (like Anthropic's Claude 3.5 Sonnet or OpenAI's GPT-4o) with a highly specific system prompt to parse the cultural nuance of the uploaded memes and generate the "Culture Breakdown."

## 8. Content

The cold-start problem is the biggest hurdle. Lingo.jpg cannot launch with an empty feed. It needs a meticulously curated database of "Seed Memes" across 2-3 starting languages (e.g., Spanish, Japanese, French). These memes need to be hand-picked for their educational value—they should teach common, highly useful slang that textbooks ignore.

**What we need to create:**

1.  **The Seed Curriculum:** 500 hand-curated memes per launch language. These need OCR extraction, literal translations, and AI-generated (but human-reviewed) Culture Breakdowns. 
2.  **The Vault Assets:** The initial batch of unlockable rewards. We need 20-30 custom, high-quality digital stickers/emojis per language. These must reference internet culture (e.g., a "This is Fine" dog wearing a sombrero, or a Pepe the Frog bowing).
3.  **The AI Prompts:** The system instructions for the Culture Breakdown AI. It needs to be trained to avoid academic language, keep explanations under 50 words, and perfectly capture the tone of the joke.
4.  **Marketing Copy:** The landing page needs to hit hard. "Stop learning how to order a train ticket. Start learning how to understand the group chat."
